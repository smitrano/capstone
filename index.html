Patagonia

Patagonia grew out of a small company that made tools for climbers. Alpinism remains at the heart of a worldwide business started by a band of climbers and surfers, and the minimalist style they promoted.

The approach taken towards product design demonstrates a bias for simplicity and utility, honoring the sense of reward that with hard-won grace and moments of connection between us and nature.

Patagonia donates time, services and at least 1% of all sales to hundreds of grassroots environmental groups all over the world who work to help reverse the tide.

For Patagonia, a love of wild and beautiful places demands participation in the fight to save them, and to help reverse the steep decline in the overall environmental health of our planet.

Workers

Patagonia extends health benefits to part-time, retail, warehouse staff, and covers 80% of health insurance premiums for full-time workers.

Why it's good for Patagonia: Studies have shown that it costs a company an average of $50,000 to replace an employee, from recruiting costs, training, and loss of productivity. A high quality health and wellness program helps Patagonia retain its skilled workers.

50% of full time employee participate in external professional development.

Why it's good for Patagonia: Providing employees with educational and professional development opportunities helps to challenge them and encourages them to build mastery in a particular domain. It can also help prepare them to move into management or other vancant internal positions.

Community

40% of management are women or ethnic minorities.

Why it's good for Patagonia: The benefits of diversity, particularly gender diversity, are well documented. McKinsey found that companies with a high representation of female executives have a 47% higher average return on equity than other businesses.

Environment

75% of materials used are environmentally preferred (organic, recycled, etc.)

Why it's good for Patagonia: As the supply of raw manufacturing materials shrinks, and the process of collecting used goods, recycled material become increasingly valuable. Using environmentally preferred materials also helps Patagonia support marketing claims and build a strong, loyal consumer base.

5% of energy use is generated on-site.

Why it's good for Patagonia: On-site energy production helps Patagonia offset production costs.

Etsy

Etsy is a marketplace that fosters human connection by bringing together creative small business owners with buyers around the world looking for unique goods with a personal story.

Etsy's heart and soul is its global community: creative entrepreneurs who run their businesses in the Etsy marketplace, shoppers looking for things they can't find anywhere else, manufacturers who partner with Etsy sellers to help them grow, and Etsy employees who build and nurture the marketplace.

Etsy's mission is to reimagine commerce in ways that build a more fulfilling and lasting world.

As of September 30, 2015, more than 1.5 million active sellers and 22.6 million active buyers used the platform in nearly every country in the world. In 2014, Etsy sellers grossed more than $1.93 billion in sales.

Workers

85% of Etsy employees answered that they were engaged in the 2014 happiness survey.

Etsy's healthcare plan covers over 80% of health insurance premiums for employees and families, and domestic partner, civil union, and/or same-sex marriage spousal benefits. They also offer employees over 60 days of paid maternity leave and over 2 weeks paid paternity leave.

Community

Etsy supports the Hacker School, a three-month, full-time program in New York that teaches people how to become better programmers.

Why it's good for Etsy: Etsy's core competency is its team of talented software developers and engineers. They recognize that there is a growing need for more talented programmers, particularly women, to meet the demands of the future. Sponsoring the Hacker School allows the company to encourage more women to join engineering at Etsy and across the industry.

Environment

Etsy tracks, publicly reports, and sets reduction goals for carbon emissions from company operations including energy use, business travel, employee commuting and shipping from the Etsy marketplace.

The company implements waste reduction programs across all offices, globally, diverting more than 34,000 pounds of waste from landfills in 2014.

Funding

Union Square Ventures, a venture capital firm that invested in Etsy (and has also invested in Foursquare, Twitter, and Zynga) says B Corps are appealing because the companies that produce the most stakeholder value over the next decade will also produce the best financial returns.

"Etsy had a spectacular public offering in April 2015. The IPO netted $267 million for the crafts marketplace, which saw its share price double on its first day of trading." - Inc. Magazine

Method

Method is the pioneer of premium environmentally-conscious and design-driven home care, fabric care and personal care products. Their mission is to inspire a healthy, happy home revolution with cleaners formulated to put the hurt on dirt without doing harm to people, creatures or our planet.

In the same way we take the dirt out of cleaning, we strive to eliminate some of business's dirty practices. We believe our business practices and processes should be as clean as the products we make.

Method develops formulas and design its products to meet the Cradle to Cradle standard, meaning they consider the past, present and future implications of everything they do.

Cradle to Cradle is a philosophy that we apply to every method product, and to the business itself. method was one of the first companies to be recognized as a Cradle to Cradle company, acknowledging our practices from the environmental quality of our products to how we manufacture them. We currently have over 60 products.

Workers

Method workers paid are paid over 40% above living wage.

Why it's good for Method: Paying employees a living wage is more beneficial than the money saved by paying them less. Goldman Sachs found that "there is a high correlation across all sectors in terms of cash flow generated relative to payroll per employee." The report goes on to say that this finding "breaks with the common prconception of improving operational efficiency through cutting payroll and, on the contrary, seems to suggest that companies that invest in their workforce will reap exponential benefits."

The company pays for the majority of health insurance premiums for workers and their families.

Why it's good for Method: This investment reduces absenteeism and turnover, and increases productivity. These gains more than cover the cost of providing these benefits.

Community

Method offers at least 20 hours paid time off for community service.

Why it's good for Method: Creating service opportunities for employees is good for business. The Center for Corporate Citizenship at Boston College reports that employee volunteer programs enhance attraction, recruitment, and skills as well as a company's image in the community. According to the study Good Companies, Better Employeers, employee volunteer programs increase positive word of mouth from employees about their employers, improve worker satisfaction, and increase employee retention rates.

Environment

Bottles are made from 100% recycled plastic.

Why it's good for Method: While the free market economist would argue that Method pays a premium for recycled material, which is true, the premium is far outweighed by the financial benefit. When oil prices spiked in 2011, nearly all of Method's competitors were forced to raise prices to cover inflated commodity costs. Because post-consumer plastic is far less carbon intense, as are most ingredients in method products, Method was able to hold its pricing. This resulted in market share gains and revenue growth 20 times the cost of the better materials. These gains have sustained, and even now with lower commodity prices, Method's recycled plastic costs essentially the same as virgin plastic.

50% of energy used comes from renewable resources.

Why it's good for Method: Method's factory is powered by 700 megawatts of wind and solar power generated onsite. Over a ten-year horizon, their energy costs will be lower than if they had bought energy from the grid.

Funding

At the time that co-founders Ryan and Lowry decided to reimagine the most prosaic of household items--cleaning supplies--large corporations like Procter & Gamble and Clorox dominated the market with fairly generic products that contained harsh, toxic chemicals. To find a foothold in the market, Method had to differentiate itself.

Method products, Ryan and Lowry decided, would be environmentally friendly and would feature elegant packaging. "Method has to enter a category with a huge disruption," Alastair Dorward, then Method's CEO, told Inc. in 2007. "The story cannot be copied overnight or eroded by existing [companies]. It has to have disruptive packaging, ingredients, and fragrance."

San Francisco Equity, prior to a 2005 spinoff from LMS Capital, had invested $475,000 in the company in 2003, and contributed another $4 million in late 2004. Simon Equity had invested around $1 million in the company in 2001, and has committed additional equity since. The company raised $25 million in committed capital, Dow Jones previousy reported. Tim Koogle, former head of Yahoo, was also a large investor.

Since SFEP's initial investment, Method's sales have grown over 400%, according to the firm. European manufacturer Ecover purchased the home-care products maker for an undisclosed sum from San Francisco Equity Partners in September 2012, a transition that co-founder Eric Ryan described as "a new chapter in the evolution of our business."

Warby Parker

A collaboration between four close friends, Warby Parker was conceived as an alternative to the overpriced and bland eyewear available today.

Prescription eyewear simply should not cost $300+. The industry is controlled by a few large companies that have kept prices artificially high, reaping huge profits from consumers who have no other options. By circumventing traditional channels and engaging with customers directly through their website, Warby Parker is able to provide high-quality, beautifully designed prescription eyewear for only $95.

Even at $95, there are almost a billion people without access to glasses, so for every pair sold, Warby Parker provides one to someone in need.

Being a B Corporation allows Warby Parker to account for the commitments they are making to their customers, their employees, the environment and the community at large.

Workers

80% of health insurance premiums covered.

Why it's good for Warby Parker:Paying employees a living wage is more beneficial than the money saved by paying them less. Goldman Sachs found that "there is a high correlation across all sectors in terms of cash flow generated relative to payroll per employee."

100% of full time employee participate in external professional development.

Why it's good for Warby Parker: Professional development encourages employees to gain additional skills that can help them fill open internal positions.

Community

One-for-one give back program benefits people in emerging markets those unable to afford glasses.

Why it's good for Warby Parker:Warby Parker's brand is built on a sense of purpose. Consumers are proud to be part of their larger mission, and eager to tell their friends about it. The one-for-one program gives Warby Parker a concrete platform of mission and purpose to build and grow its brand from.

Environment

Warby Parker is carbon neutral.

Why it's good for Warby Parker: Proving its commitment to the environment through action helps back up Warby Parker's brand promise, while making production processes more efficient.

The company conducts an annual internal envionmental review.

Why it's good for Warby Parker: Annual audits formalize the company's commitment to its social and envionmental mission, and integrate it into company culture.

Funding

Warby Parker has raised a total of $215 million from investors including Spark Capital and credit-card company American Express Co. Its board of directors includes Mickey Drexler, the CEO of J. Crew and a board member at Apple.

The company has more than doubled its valuation since its last funding round in December 2013, when investors valued the company at $500 million, according to a report in Fortune. - NY Times
